        <label for="tribute-info" class="bold">A Timeline of Her Life:</label>
        <p><span class="bold">March 1822: </span>Born in Dorchester County, Maryland.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">Late 1830s: </span>Tubman experienced an accidental, severe head injury after an overseer threw a weight meant for another enslaved person and missed, hitting her in the head. This led to lifelong health issues including seizures and horrible headaches.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">1844: </span>She married a free Black man named John Tubman.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">1849: </span>She escaped enslavement and returned to Maryland to rescue her family. This is also when she began rescuing other enslaved people.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">December 1850:</span> Tubman helped her niece and her family avoid being sold away and escape to Philadelphia.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">1858: </span> She began working with fellow abolitionist, John Brown. Tubman helped him plan a raid on Harpers Ferry, Virginia.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">1863: </span> She became "the first woman to lead an armed assault during the Civil War". </p>
        <p><span class="bold">1874: </span> A bill was introduced in the House of Representatives to pay Tubman "the sum of $2,000 for services rendered by her to the Union Army as scout, nurse, and spy", but it unfortunately did not pass in the Senate.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">Late 1890s: </span> Tubman got involved in women's suffrage, traveling around the country and speaking out in support.</p>
        <p><span class="bold">March 10, 1913:</span> Passed away in Auburn, New York.</p>
      </section>
